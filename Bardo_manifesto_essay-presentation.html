The Trillion-Dollar Blind Spot

Manifesto / Essay

Trillions flow toward sustainability, but almost nobody can prove what that money actually does. That gap isn't just an inefficiency. It's the thing holding the entire transition back.

Oskar Klingberg

Why this gap exists

It's tempting to point fingers. Just 36 fossil fuel companies are responsible for half the world's carbon emissions.

But that framing misses something important.

Billions are pouring into climate right now. When my co-founder CJ meets with investors backing these companies, the belief is real. The money is real. But the same question keeps coming up: how do you know which ones are actually delivering? When every company has a sustainability story, how do you tell the difference between real results and a fancy report?

Companies are under mounting pressure to choose suppliers with lower emissions. But when a supplier says they've reduced their carbon footprint by 30%, how do you verify that? With what data? Measured how?

It's like choosing a restaurant based solely on their self-written Google reviews.

What changes when you can actually measure

Think about what GPS did to logistics. Before it, shipping was slow, expensive, and full of guesswork. After it, routes optimised, costs dropped, and entirely new businesses became possible. GPS didn't move a single package. It just made the system visible.

And visibility changed everything.

The same thing happens when carbon becomes measurable. Capital stops flowing to the best storytellers and starts flowing to the best performers. Sustainability stops being a cost centre and becomes a competitive edge.

Why Stockholm. Why now.

Sweden, a country of ten million people, is producing more world-class sustainability companies per capita than anywhere on earth. Stegra, Einride, Syre, Blykalla. Fossil-free steel, autonomous electric freight, textile-to-textile recycling, small modular nuclear. Billions in capital backing them.

And then there's Northvolt. It raised more than any of them. It was supposed to be Europe's answer to CATL and Tesla's battery supply chain. It didn't make it.

Northvolt didn't fail because the mission was wrong. It failed because the systems to track, verify, and course-correct in real time weren't there. When billions are at stake, you can't afford to discover your blind spots after the money's been spent.

Every one of these companies faces the same question: how do you know it's actually working?

Why Bardo

Bardo started inside Stegra. When they set out to build the world's first fossil-free steel plant, they wanted to prove it was green. Down to every supplier, every input, every transaction. The tools to do that didn't exist.

Most carbon data out there today is guesswork. Industry averages. Spend-based estimates. That's like a doctor skipping your test results and treating patients based on national health statistics.

Bardo works at the transaction level. Invoice by invoice, line by line. Not estimates, but actual emissions from actual activities.

When you can see carbon at that resolution, you don't just find your footprint. You find your waste. The expensive supplier who's also the dirty one. It becomes both a sustainability tool and a business tool.

That's what we're building. Not another reporting dashboard. An intelligence layer that makes carbon as visible and actionable as financial data.

What we're looking for

The infrastructure that the world's economy will run on in twenty years is being built right now. Not all of it, but the critical layers. The ones that determine whether the transition actually happens or just gets talked about.

The window for building the definitive company in this space is open now, driven by regulation, pulled by market demand, and enabled by technology that wasn't possible five years ago.

We're looking for people who want to work on something that matters at a scale that matters. Engineers who want to solve hard data problems with real consequences. Commercial minds who want to sell something that companies need, not something they have to be convinced to want. And partners and investors who understand that the next great European platform company might not come from payments or music, it might come from the most important dataset the business world has ever had to get right.

If that sounds like something worth your time, we should talk.
